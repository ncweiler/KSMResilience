In the fall of 2012, the storm surge caused by Hurricane Sandy devastated the U.S. East Coast, causing particularly severe damage to urban areas in New York and New Jersey.

The estimated damages to these areas cost over 20 billion dollars.

In Sandy's aftermath, analysis revealed that much of the damage could have been reduced by investing in comparatively cheaper structural mitigation measures.

Developing strong infrastructure to combat storm surges is vital for creating resilient coastal communities.

Resilience begins with home owners, who can take small structural mitigation measures to ensure their house is capable of withstanding the threat of natural hazards.

Coalitions that are comprised of multiple stakeholders play a key role in the development of new resilience measures.

The "Rebuild By Design" project, part of the president's Hurricane Sandy rebuilding task force, brought together ten design teams and challenged them to come up with innovative resilience measures for the NY/NJ region. "On the Water/Palisade Bay" is a collaboration of engineers, city planners and students, brought together to imagine new approaches to mitigate the threat of rising seas.

Their vision highlights the role of green infrastructure, which aims to combine storm mitigation measures with environmental enhancement.

The illustrations below demonstrate some of the many benefits of green infrastructure and mitigation measures.

Ecosystems like coastal wetlands have the ability to move landward as sea level rises. However, physical obstacles such as seawalls can block the advancement of these wetlands, potentially leading to shoreline erosion.

Natural coastal habitats, such as oyster reefs, tidal marshes and seagrass, can protect coastal areas by reducing wave-induced erosion and inundation. Maintaining a healthy coastal habitat, through restoration or conservation can greatly increase the resilience of a coastal community.

The vulnerability of coastal communities can vary greatly depending on their coastal habitat. Communities with none of these natural defenses are at a higher risk for damage from a storm surge. Investments in green infrastructure like oyster beds or restoration of tidal marshes can make a big difference in coastal communities.
