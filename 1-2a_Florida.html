Building For the Next Storm (Florida)

In August of 1992, Florida was sturck by a category 5 hurricane, devastating local communities.

Hurricane Andrew destroyed over 25,000 homes and damaged at least 100,000 more.

A study in the aftermath of Andrew revealed that not all of the damage was inevitable. Had Florida properly enforced its building codes, one-third of the damage could have been avoided.

The damage of Andrew inspired Florida to rethink, strengthen and enforce their building codes.

Starting in 1995, new coastal homes were required to include design features to help them withstand high winds.

At the same time, home builders and officials were trained so that they could implement the new building standards.

In 2002, Florida adopted a new statewide building code and ensured that all licensed engineers, architects and contractors were trained in all of its requirements.

These new codes were tested in 2004 with the arrival of Hurricane Charley and three other hurricanes in the span of six weeks.

In the area hit hardest, Charlotte County, a study revealed that residents whose homes were built after 1996, when the new standards were implemented, filed 60 percent fewer claims than those whose homes had been built earlier.

When the post-1996 home owners who did file claims, they were 42 percent less severe than the claims for the older homes.

The improved bulding codes in Florida increased coastal communities' hurricane resilience and demonstrate how small investments in structural improvements can pay off in the long run.
