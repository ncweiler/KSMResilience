Building For the Next Storm

In August of 2005, Hurricane Katrina made landfall, causing billions of dollars in damage and displacing hundreds of thousands of Gulf Coast residents.

For the 8,000 Vietnamese residents of a tightly-knit community in New Orleans East, this was an opportunity to rebuild their community even stronger than before.

Their strong sense of community and sense of hope in the face of adversity contributed to their incredible resilience as a group.

On the eve of the storm, the community organized evacuation plans through the local Catholic church and a local radio station.

Afterwards, the church leader and community volunteers took boats through flooded neighborhoods to check on everyone in the community.

Through the coordinated efforts of the community, the fatalities were limited to just a single elderly resident.

Since then, more than 90 percent of the Vietnamese community has returned to New Orleans East, a higher percentage than most other groups in the area.

After weathering the effects of Katrina, the residents of New Orleans East were poised to band together if other disasters should arise.

When the 2010 Deepwater Horizon oil spill destroyed the local seafood industry on which many residents relied, the community stepped up again.

They came together and developed a new, green aquaponics project with the surrounding community to supplement their economy while the Gulf recovered.

Some disasters cannot be avoided, but a resilient community will find new ways to adapt.
